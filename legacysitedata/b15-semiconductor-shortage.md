In this week's blog, we try to understand why we're amid a Global Semiconductor Shortage, and how shortages have been hurting manufacturers of automobiles, laptops, and consumer electronics. Mobile phone companies are paying through the roof to source critical parts.

The global chip shortage came as sudden news to many investors. The delay, affecting products from cars to smartphones, has been looming for some time, driven by trade restrictions, offshoring, and the global Covid-19 pandemic.

## High global demand amid a pandemic

When Covid-19 hit China, factories shuttered, producing a slowdown in manufacturing. Then, demand swung — there was unprecedented demand for PCs, cameras, keyboards, and displays. People who had PCs realized they needed better versions; multi-person households could no longer share devices. PC demand grew "10% growth in the PC space for 2020, and surging demand in the fourth quarter reached 25.4%."

Behavioral shifts moved dollars from venues to home entertainment. Smart TVs, streaming services, and gaming consoles all surged. Tablets had their strongest demand since 2015, with "over 160 million units sold in 2020." Chromebook sales rose from less than 4 million to over 11 million. Smart TVs went from less than 4 million in Q4 2019 to nearly 5 million a year later.

## Difficult to forecast during uncertain times

Smartphone forecasts predicted a slowdown but business returned to normal by Q3 2020. Auto and electronics makers cut back drastically and now rush to re-up orders. In a fabless world, those cuts led to gaping holes in supply.

By Q4, demand surged to nearly pre-pandemic levels (down by only 2%). The 5G cycle, including Apple's 5G iPhone, further spurred demand. Automakers warned of shortages late 2020 after demand picked up post-shutdown.

## Geopolitical factors

Trump administration regulations on chip sales to Huawei, ZTE, and other Chinese firms led those companies to stockpile chips. American firms were cut off from China's SMIC chips after the firm was blacklisted.

## So when will the shortage be resolved?

Asian chip factories are ramping up production, but warned increases won't be instant. Chips can take up to "26 weeks" to produce; new fabs take up to three years and roughly "$10 billion each."

Bottom line: the semiconductor shortage is pulling multiple industries to its knees.
